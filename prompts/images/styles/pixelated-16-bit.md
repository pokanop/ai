# Pixelated / 16-bit

[← Back to Image Prompts](../README.md)

Authentic SNES/Genesis-era pixel art with strictly limited color palettes, visible pixel grids, and the charming constraints of 16-bit hardware. Every pixel is intentionally placed, every color carefully selected from a restricted palette. The style encompasses everything from platformer sprites to detailed pixel-art scenes, capturing the nostalgic magic of gaming's golden age — vibrant, precise, and instantly recognizable.

**Best for:** Game assets · Profile pictures · Desktop wallpapers · Social media posts · Twitch/YouTube banners · Sticker designs · Merchandise

Detailed pixel-level shading using dithering patterns rather than smooth gradients. Each tile and sprite is pixel-perfect. The moon casts a visible pixel-shadow from the pagoda.
> ```

## 💡 Tips & Best Practices

- **"Visible pixel grid"**: Without this, AI models anti-alias the pixels into smooth edges. Always insist on visible, crisp pixel boundaries.
- **Limit the palette explicitly**: "16-color palette" or "32-color palette" forces the AI to work within authentic constraints. More colors dilute the retro feel.
- **Dithering is the shading**: "Dithering patterns rather than smooth gradients" defines how 16-bit art handles tonal transitions — alternating pixels of two colors to create the illusion of a third.
- **Name the hardware**: "SNES / Super Nintendo" or "Sega Genesis" or "Game Boy" — each has a distinct aesthetic and color set. The hardware reference calibrates the AI.
- **Common pitfalls**: "Pixel art" alone often produces modern, high-color pixel art that lacks retro charm. Always constrain the palette and name the hardware era.
