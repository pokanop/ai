# Tilt-Shift Miniature Effect

[← Back to Image Prompts](../README.md)

Real-world scenes photographed (or processed to appear as if) with a tilt-shift lens that creates a narrow band of sharp focus, making full-size scenes look like tiny scale models. Cities become model train layouts, highways become slot-car tracks, and people become tiny figurines. The effect is amplified by slightly oversaturated colors and careful composition — shooting from above at a steep angle.

**Best for:** Desktop wallpapers · Social media posts · Travel photography · Urban photography · Art prints · Aerial views

![Tilt-Shift Example](../../../assets/placeholder-tilt-shift.jpeg)

> **Sample prompt used to generate the above image (Nano Banana 2):**
> ```text
> Tilt-shift photograph of a busy European city square from a high elevated angle, 16:9 landscape format. The miniature effect is aggressive — only a narrow horizontal band in the center is in sharp focus, everything above and below is dramatically blurred. Slightly oversaturated colors make the red buses, yellow taxis, and green park trees look like model-paint colors. The tiny pedestrians look like painted figurines. Bright midday sunlight with crisp shadows. The scene genuinely appears to be a photograph of a meticulously detailed scale model rather than a real city.
> ```

## 💡 Tips & Best Practices

- **Shoot from above**: Tilt-shift miniature effect ONLY works from an elevated angle looking down. Eye-level breaks the illusion.
- **Narrow focus band**: The narrower the focus, the stronger the miniature effect. "Only a narrow horizontal band in sharp focus."
- **Oversaturate slightly**: "Slightly oversaturated colors" makes objects look model-painted — this is critical for the miniature illusion.
- **Bright lighting**: Crisp midday sunlight with clear shadows reinforces the model-photography look. Overcast or dim lighting weakens it.
- **Common pitfalls**: Eye-level tilt-shift doesn't look miniature. Dark/moody lighting fights the model aesthetic.
